01 · The Central Rule

The one sentence everything else hangs from.

Every framework on this page is a way of keeping this sentence true when the code gets complicated. If you remember nothing else from four weeks, remember this — and remember that it is a statement about people, not about technology.

The Bot is the GPS.
The Human is the Driver.

A GPS is brilliant: it knows every road, it never gets tired, it answers instantly. It also cannot see the flood on the road ahead, and it is sometimes confidently wrong. It informs. It never drives. Your bot tells people what it knows; humans decide what to do.

What it permits

Inform brilliantly

Answer the same question at 2 AM as at 2 PM, in plain language, correctly for the island the person is actually on. Look things up. Explain. Guide, step by step.

What it forbids

Decide, promise, approve

The bot may create a case; it may never approve one. It may find the form; it may never grant the permit. It may point to the desk; it may never promise the outcome.

Why it holds

Confidence ≠ correctness

A model can be 100% fluent and 100% wrong, and it will sound exactly the same either way. That is precisely why a human stays in the driver's seat.

02 · The Three Axes

A.R.T. — Authority, Register, Territory.

Three ways a bot fails a real person, and therefore three things every camp bot is tested against. You'll meet Authority on Day 6, Register on Day 7, Territory on Day 8 — and all three get attacked on Day 9.

Axis 1 · Day 6

Authority

What is this bot allowed to do? The moment a message says my ticket, my refund, my application, my child — the bot stops answering and starts escalating.

  • No personal record lookups
  • No approvals, no promises
  • No collecting payment details
  • Case creation is the ceiling of its power

Coded as the gate: is_case_specific() — the Bouncer at the door.

Axis 2 · Day 7

Register

The same facts, in the right voice. A hyped teen, an anxious grandmother at midnight, a vex customer and a panicked parent need four different deliveries of identical information.

  • Arrival state decides the outfit
  • Facts must not change when tone does
  • Cheerfulness at the wrong moment is a failure
  • Derived from your empathy maps, never invented

Coded as classify_register() — the Wardrobe.

Axis 3 · Day 8

Territory

Eight islands, eight rulebooks. "When does Carnival start?" has eight different true answers. A bot that assumes one island is a bot that sends a family to the wrong event on the wrong week.

  • Ask where before answering
  • Never fold one island's rules into another's
  • Ambiguity earns a clarifying question
  • Diaspora time zones count as territory too

The failure that ends up on the radio call-in show.

03 · The Prompt Frame

TCRDEI — six letters, one good prompt.

Vibes produce inconsistent bots. This frame produces prompts you can debug. T, C and D live permanently in your system prompt; R usually joins them; E and I are what you do every time you test.

T
Thoughtfully framed task
Give the bot a name, an employer and one specific job. "You are FestPass AI, a support agent for FestPass ECCU Ltd." A bot that knows who it works for stops improvising.
C
Context supplied
Who is on the other end, and what do they already know? This is where your persona work enters the prompt — the arrival state, the island, the tech comfort.
R
Reference examples
Two or three In→Out pairs teach your style better than a page of adjectives. Made-up data only — never a real customer, never a real case.
D
Defined deliverables
Exactly what the bot may hand over — dates, venues, price tiers, payment steps — and the shape of the answer: concise, numbered, text-first.
E
Evaluate the output
Read what came back like a QA officer, not a proud parent. Did it hold the red line? Did the facts survive the tone change? Did it guess an island?
I
Iterate
Sharpen one line, run it again. ABI — Always Be Iterating. Nobody's first prompt is their best prompt; that's not failure, that's the method.
04 · The Persona Anchor

Your bot's DNA, in four lines.

This is the shape every Studio's master system prompt takes. It goes in Google AI Studio's System Instructions box — not the chat box — where the model reads it before every single reply. Copy the skeleton, never the skin: every gold field must come from your client's work.

You are [a very specific role — e.g. a warm, plain-language assistant for the ASPIRE programme in St. Kitts & Nevis]. Your job is [the outcome — inform, guide, escalate]. You NEVER [your red lines — answer personal-case questions, reveal anyone's information, invent facts]. Instead you [escalate to a named human desk, with a reference]. Your tone is [a register from YOUR wardrobe — warm · patient · practical · urgent].

05 · The Empathy-to-Prompt Canvas

Persona → arrival state → register → prompt.

The whole method, left to right. Every prompt you write should be traceable back to a real observation about a real kind of person. If you can't trace it, you invented it — and invented users produce bots nobody wants to talk to.

1
Persona
A well-drawn character built from patterns of real users — never a real person, never real data. Done when you can hear them type.
2
Arrival state
How they show up: hyped, careful, time-poor, aggrieved, panicked — and the good ones: proud, grateful, loyal. Positive arrivals deserve engineered answers too.
3
Register
The outfit the bot wears to meet that state. Same facts, different delivery. Your wardrobe is derived from your maps, one register per arrival state.
4
Prompt
The instructions that make it happen, in TCRDEI shape, anchored by the Persona Anchor. Then evaluate, iterate, attack your own red lines.
The gap that matters most

SAYS vs THINKS

Zari types "how much is j'ouvert" — she's thinking "if this bot lectures me I'm closing it." People almost never say the thing they fear. The empathy map is how you hear the quiet part, and the quiet part is where good bots are designed.

The reward loop

Notice → name → reward

A register can celebrate as deliberately as it de-escalates. Catch the good arrival — a first Carnival, a proud provider, twenty-two years at the same stall — and name it. Recognised, rewarded, returned: a bot that only handles problems teaches people to bring it only problems.

07 · The Lines

Red lines protect trust. Green lines build it.

Every persona has both: a thing your bot must never do, and a promise it keeps every single time. A bot that only knows its nevers is safe. A bot that also keeps its always-promises is loved.

Red lines · NEVER

The hard limits

  • Never collect or reveal a minor's personal details
  • Never discuss payment status, balances or account records
  • Never guess a deadline, a fee or a rule — escalate instead
  • Never promise a lost child is found or safe
  • Never argue, and never quote policy at someone like a wall
  • Never speculate beyond the official release
  • Never send an image-only answer to someone who can't load it
Green lines · ALWAYS

The promises

  • Always state age rules kindly, up front, with exact prices
  • Always confirm you're official and number the steps
  • Always lead with the deadline, then the plain checklist
  • Always open with one calm sentence and the exact desk
  • Always acknowledge first, with a case reference in the first exchange
  • Always cite the source, with its date
  • Always answer text-first, front-loaded, bundled
08 · Glossary

The words your facilitators use.

Camp vocabulary and the industry terms behind it. Type to filter — if a word gets said in a stand-up and you don't have it, it belongs here; tell a facilitator and we'll add it.

System prompt
The standing order the model reads before every reply. Your bot's DNA — one paragraph, every answer.Also: system instructions
Persona Anchor
The camp's system-prompt template: role · job · NEVER · tone. Pinned on top of every call.
Register
The voice the bot answers in, chosen by how the user arrived. Same facts, different outfit.Axis 2 · classify_register()
Arrival state
The emotional state a user shows up in — hyped, anxious, aggrieved, panicked, proud. Registers are derived from these.
Empathy map
Four quadrants (says · thinks · feels · does) plus goals, pains, needs and questions — how you observe a user instead of guessing at them.
Job to be Done
The one sentence starting "Help me…" that names what a user actually came for. Miss it and a polite answer is still a failed answer.
Red line
A thing your bot must never do. Written by you, attacked by you, on purpose.
Green line
A promise your bot keeps every time. The positive twin of a red line.
Escalation
Handing a person to a named human desk with a reference — never dropping them. Firm rule, warm delivery.
The Bouncer
The authority gate at the door of every answer: is this question case-specific? If yes, escalate.Axis 1 · is_case_specific()
The Wardrobe
Your set of registers, one per arrival state. Kept in a TONES dictionary in Python.
Territory
Which island's rules apply. Eight territories, eight rulebooks — ask where before you answer.Axis 3
TCRDEI
Task · Context · Reference examples · Deliverables · Evaluate · Iterate. The camp's prompt frame.
ABI
Always Be Iterating. Your first prompt is a draft, not a verdict.
Model
Which AI brain answers you. Different models, different strengths, different costs.
Temperature
The creativity dial. Low = focused and predictable; high = creative and unpredictable. Keep it near the middle.
Token
The word-pieces a model reads and writes. Long conversations cost more of them — which is why concise is a design choice, not just a style.
Hallucination
When the model states something false with total confidence. The reason the human stays in the driver's seat.
Grounding
Tying the bot's answers to source material it's allowed to trust, instead of its own memory.
Guardrail
Anything that stops the bot doing harm — a prompt rule, a code gate, a filter, a human in the loop.
Red team
Attacking your own bot on purpose to find what breaks, before a real user finds it by accident.Day 9 · Crash Test
Crash Test
The camp's structured red-team session: log what you typed, what it said, and which line broke.
PII
Personally identifiable information — real names, addresses, account or card numbers. Never typed into an AI, ever.
Studio & Pod
Your Studio is your country team on its client; your Pod is your small build team inside it. Roles rotate Mondays.
Demonstration client
FestPass ECCU Ltd. — fictional, shared by every Studio. The flight simulator you learn on before touching the real client's controls.
MVP
Minimum viable product — the smallest version that genuinely works. What ships at the end of Week 4.

No match — try a shorter word, or ask a facilitator to add the term.
